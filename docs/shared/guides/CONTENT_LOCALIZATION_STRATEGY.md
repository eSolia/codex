## Core Concept: Audience-Specific Content Adaptation

Beyond simple translation, we create audience-aware content that recognizes the different contexts and needs of Japanese vs. international readers operating in Japan's business environment.

## The "Not 1:1" Principle

**English and Japanese content do not need to be mirror translations of each other.** The same service page can ‚Äî and often should ‚Äî take a completely different approach in each language:

- **Different openings.** JA might lead with "explaining to headquarters why Japan is different." EN might lead with "here's what you need to know as a foreigner in Japan."
- **Different emphasis.** JA readers already understand ringi and hanko ‚Äî they need help framing these for overseas colleagues. EN readers need the concept explained from scratch.
- **Different structure.** A comparison table might work in EN where a narrative paragraph works better in JA, or vice versa.
- **Different length.** If the JA version needs more context to set up the "explaining to HQ" framing, let it be longer. If the EN version can be shorter because the reader doesn't need cultural background, let it be shorter.
- **Shared skeleton.** Frontmatter fields, HTML blocks (cards, grids), and page structure should stay aligned so the site renders consistently. The _prose content_ within that skeleton is what adapts.

When reviewing bilingual pairs, the question is not "does the JA say the same thing as the EN?" but "does each version serve its audience effectively?"

## The "Ally/Bridge" Model

### Japanese Readers: Cultural Bridge Position

**Context**: Japanese professionals in global companies often need to:

- Explain Japan's unique requirements to overseas colleagues
- Report to global headquarters in acceptable formats
- Reconcile local compliance with international standards
- Advocate for Japan-specific processes within global frameworks

**Content Approach**:

- Position eSolia as a partner who understands local complexity
- Give readers concrete language for explaining Japan's requirements to HQ
- Focus on global integration and reporting
- Emphasize bilingual bridge services
- Frame eSolia as the translator between Japan and headquarters

### International Readers: Educational Context

**Context**: Non-Japanese professionals operating in Japan often need:

- Background on why Japan is different from their home countries
- Step-by-step guidance through unfamiliar regulatory requirements
- Comparative context with systems they understand
- Practical implications for their business operations
- Cultural and linguistic support

**Content Approach**:

- Explain historical/cultural reasons for unique systems
- Provide comparative context ("Unlike most countries...")
- Detail practical implications and challenges
- Offer step-by-step guidance
- Address common frustrations and confusion points

## Content Categories for Localization Review

### HIGH PRIORITY: Japan-Specific Regulatory Services

These services involve unique Japanese requirements that international readers need education about, while Japanese readers need help explaining to global stakeholders.

1. **Security/Compliance Services**
   - Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA) compliance
   - My Number Act requirements
   - J-SOX IT controls
   - Japanese vendor integration (SECOM, ALSOK)

2. **Infrastructure Services**
   - Office moves (building regulations, utilities)
   - Telephone systems (NTT, carrier relationships)
   - Physical security (Japanese standards)
   - Data center requirements

3. **Process/Legal Services**
   - Consulting methodologies in Japanese context
   - Project management with Japanese stakeholders
   - Outsourcing regulations and practices

### MEDIUM PRIORITY: Technical Services with Local Nuances

Technical services that have Japanese-specific implementation requirements or vendor relationships.

4. **Cloud/Platform Services**
   - Google Workspace (Japanese features, local support)
   - Microsoft 365 (Japan-specific compliance, local data centers)
   - PROdb (local development, Japanese business practices)

5. **Managed Services**
   - Helpdesk (bilingual support expectations)
   - On-site support (Japanese business etiquette)
   - Training (cultural adaptation requirements)

### LOWER PRIORITY: Universal Technical Services

Services that are similar globally but may benefit from cultural context.

6. **General IT Services**
   - Website design (Japanese UX preferences)
   - Localization (beyond translation to cultural adaptation)

## Content Localization Checklist

### For Japanese Versions:

- [ ] Frame as supporting global enterprises
- [ ] Include "explaining to overseas colleagues" language
- [ ] Focus on global headquarters reporting
- [ ] Emphasize bilingual bridge value
- [ ] Address international audit compliance
- [ ] Position readers as cultural advocates

### For English Versions:

- [ ] Provide historical/cultural context for Japanese uniqueness
- [ ] Include comparative language ("Unlike most countries...")
- [ ] Detail practical implications for international businesses
- [ ] Address common frustrations/confusion points
- [ ] Offer step-by-step guidance through complex processes
- [ ] Explain "why" Japan does things differently
